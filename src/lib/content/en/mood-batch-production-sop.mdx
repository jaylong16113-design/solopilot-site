# SOP for One-Person Teams: Producing 5 Mood Videos a Day From Topic Selection to Publishing in Under 2 Hours

I spent a long time figuring out one problem: how can one person produce 3-5 mood videos every single day?

Not the lazy transition-to-beat filler videos. Real videos that move people and create emotional resonance. At first, I thought it was impossible — write the script, find the footage, record the voiceover, edit, publish. Even at a fast pace, one video takes 1-2 hours. Three videos means 3-6 hours, and the quality drops fast.

Then I realized it wasn't a time problem. It was a process problem. Assembly-line batch production and handcrafting one video at a time are completely different logics. The SOP below is what I settled on after two months of testing. Today, I average 4 mood videos per day, spending under 2 hours on content creation.

## The Full SOP at a Glance

Here's the overall flow. I'll break down each step next.

| Stage | Time | Tools | Output |
|:------|:-----|:------|:-------|
| Topic planning | 15 min | Feishu/Excel + Trending Topics | Weekly topic sheet |
| Script generation | 30 min (4 scripts) | ChatGPT/Claude + Prompt templates | 4 complete scripts |
| Footage sourcing | 20 min (4 videos) | Kling / Pexels / Phone camera | Visual footage |
| AI voiceover | 5 min (4 videos) | ElevenLabs / CapCut voiceover | Audio files |
| Editing & export | 40 min (4 videos) | CapCut + templates | 4 finished videos |
| Publishing & distribution | 10 min | Feishu bot / Manual | Live on each platform |

Total: roughly 2 hours, producing 4 emotional short videos. Note: this efficiency comes after practice. When you're just starting, each video might take an hour. But once the workflow clicks, efficiency jumps fast.

Let me break down each step.

## Topic Planning: 15 Minutes to Lock In a Week's Worth of Topics

This is the most critical step — and the one most people skip. What's the biggest risk in batch production? Running out of ideas by the third video. You have to lock in your topics beforehand.

My method: every weekend, I spend 15 minutes building a simple table in Feishu. I use only three topic sources:

1. **Comment keywords.** Go through the comments on your last 10 best-performing videos. Write down the most frequent words. For example: "social anxiety," "overtime," "breakup," "alone." These become the core of next week's topics.

2. **Seasonal emotions** (refer to my content calendar article). "May breakup season," "June graduation" — the calendar itself hands you topics.

3. **Trending emotional points.** Scan Weibo and Douyin trending boards for emotional hotspots. Note down the keywords.

For each topic, I only need 5 keywords and one core emotion. No full script at this stage — that's step two. For example:

- Topic 1: Loneliness of coming home late from work | empty subway, streetlights, cold takeout, dead phone, zoning out at home
- Topic 2: The ache of a secret crush | pretending to run into them, saving chat records, reading into everything, afraid to confess, seeing them in dreams

Five topics per week is the sweet spot. If you batch-produce on Saturdays, you can publish one per day Monday through Friday, with a rest day or an extra mid-week post.

## Script Generation: 30 Minutes for 4 Scripts

Got your keywords and emotional direction? Now use AI to batch-write the scripts. The key here isn't letting AI free-associate — it's using structured prompts to lock the script into the right format.

Here's my prompt template:

```
You're an emotional short video copywriter. Write a voiceover script for an emotional short video.

Topic: {emotion topic, e.g. "secret crush"}
Keywords: {keywords extracted earlier}
Length: 200-250 characters

Structure requirements:
- Opening (3-5 sentences): Build immersion with one line, typically starting with "Have you ever…" or "Have you felt like…"
- Middle (6-10 sentences): Emotional progression using specific details and scenes
- Closing (2-3 sentences): A lingering line that leaves people thinking

Style requirements: Conversational, like one person talking to another. No formal words like "however," "therefore," or "firstly."
```

With this prompt, both ChatGPT and Claude can output a solid script in 1-2 minutes. Read it over, tweak anything off, and you're done in about 30 seconds per script. Four scripts including generation and adjustments: 25-30 minutes.

If you're doing the purely visual Silent Diary style, the script is even simpler — just emotional prompts with no full voiceover. Example: "A girl sitting by the window on a subway seat, raindrops streaking across the glass, staring into space." These take 10 seconds to write. You can crank out 10 in 5 minutes.

## Footage Sourcing: 20 Minutes to Cover 4 Videos

Three source options, ranked by efficiency:

**Option 1: AI-generated footage (fastest)**
Generate visuals directly from your script using Kling, Midjourney, or Runway. Each second of video needs roughly 1-2 images or short clips. For a 20-second mood video, aim for 10-15 different visuals. Batch-generate in Midjourney or Kling: feed in the scene descriptions from your script, generate 4 images at a time, pick the best ones.

My practical tip: start with a "universal footage library" strategy. Many mood video shots are reusable — rain-streaked windows, empty seats, lamp-lit streets, a refrigerator door opening and closing. Save these "evergreen shots" in a footage library. A library of 30 universal images covers 80% of typical mood video needs.

**Option 2: Free stock footage sites**
Pexels and Pixabay have surprisingly rich emotional footage. Search in English for better results: keywords like "lonely," "rain," "window," "sad," "thinking." I use Pexels regularly — its AI search feature is quite useful.

**Option 3: Shoot your own (most time-consuming, not recommended)**
Unless you already have footage sitting on your phone or camera from past shoots, don't go out specifically to film for every single video. Whether the shooting effort is worth it depends on your quality standards. During the batch production phase, skip this path.

## AI Voiceover and Editing: 45 Minutes to Finished Videos

For voiceover, I use ElevenLabs or CapCut's AI voice function. ElevenLabs has better quality — especially for emotional narration — its voices carry real feeling. But CapCut's free version is sufficient. Just pick a fitting voice tone.

Key settings: Slow down the speech rate to 0.9-1.0x (normal or slightly slower). Emotional voiceover loses its impact when it's too fast. Add elements like sighs, pauses, and breath sounds — a huge part of what makes mood videos feel real is those tiny pauses and the sound of breathing.

CapCut batch editing workflow:

1. Create a "master" timeline. Set your usual canvas ratio (9:16 vertical), font style, subtitle position, and default transitions.
2. Import all your footage. Drag clips onto the timeline. Arrange them according to your script's rhythm.
3. Import your voiceover file. Line up the footage with the audio.
4. Add BGM. For emotional content, pick slow piano or strings. Drop the volume to 20-30%.
5. Export in one click.

If you're producing multiple similar videos, CapCut's "duplicate draft" feature is incredibly useful. Finish one video, duplicate the draft, swap out the footage and voiceover, tweak the BGM, and your second video is done. Four videos in about 30-40 minutes.

## Publishing and Distribution: 10 Minutes

The key to the publishing stage: "different versions for different platforms" (refer to my article on video length). Here's my approach:

1. Cut each video into three length versions: 25 seconds (Douyin/Xiaohongshu), 45 seconds (WeChat Video/Shorts), 60 seconds (full version as backup). The cutting time increases slightly, but distribution effectiveness doubles.

2. Different platforms prefer different titles. Douyin likes them short (8-15 characters). Xiaohongshu needs keywords (15-25 characters). WeChat Video wants an emotional hook (10-20 characters). Don't use the same title everywhere.

3. Scheduled publishing. My habit: one post at noon and one at 8 PM on weekdays. These two time slots consistently deliver the highest engagement rates for emotional content.

If you're running multiple accounts, the advantage compounds. But if you're just one person, focus on one account and publish one scheduled video per day.

## Can You Use This SOP Right Now?

Yes — but three conditions apply:

First, get your single-video workflow right before scaling up. Don't jump straight into batch production with this SOP. Make sure your individual video quality is solid first. Once you've made one or two videos that performed well and you have a feel for the entire chain, batch production won't steer you off course.

Second, low efficiency in the first two weeks is normal. The first time I ran this workflow, 4 videos took me nearly 5 hours. Now I average 2 hours. Between then and now, I made a ton of optimizations — saved my reusable prompts as templates, built a stock footage library, learned CapCut's batch functions. All of this takes time and experience.

Third, the goal of batch production isn't quantity — it's consistent output. You can't make 5 videos one day and zero the next. But you can reliably produce 20 videos per week. A steady publishing rhythm matters far more than one big blowout. Staying visible in the algorithm and in users' feeds consistently beats chasing single-viral bursts.

The honest truth: this SOP will dramatically improve your efficiency. But the core of a mood video is still "does it actually move anyone?" Efficiency is useless if your scripts aren't genuine and your footage doesn't resonate. The underlying logic of this SOP isn't "overwhelm with quantity." It's "free up your creative energy so you can focus on polishing the emotional core of every single video."
