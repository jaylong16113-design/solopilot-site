# The 6 Emotions That Make Short-Form Videos Go Viral: A Psychological Breakdown of Hit Content

Have you ever noticed that the videos you can't stop scrolling through all tap into the same handful of emotions? I've been making emotional short videos for over six months, and the first three months were pure guesswork — chasing trends, copying what was hot. The results were either lukewarm or had great view counts but comments sections full of arguments.

Then I started digging into Paul Ekman's theory of six basic emotions. That's when it clicked: viral videos aren't about fancy effects, slick editing, or clever copywriting. They're about precisely triggering one of these core emotions.

These six emotions power 90% of viral content on Douyin and WeChat Video. This isn't speculation — I broke down hundreds of videos myself.

## Joy — The Emotion Most Likely to Get Shared

Joy-driven videos share one trait: they make you feel better after watching. Think cute pets, children's unfiltered moments, or strangers being kind to each other.

**The formula:** Set up a "small daily frustration" → Create a reversal → End on a warm note

I once made a video that opened with: "Got yelled at by my boss today. On my way home, a stray cat was waiting by my scooter." It showed the cat following me home, and ended with: "It didn't seem to have anywhere else to go, so... I guess we live together now." Set to gentle BGM. That video had the highest share rate of anything I'd ever posted — because joy is the emotion people most want to spread.

**Music:** Bright piano or guitar, ~120 bpm
**Visuals:** Warm tones, natural light, minimal editing

## Sadness — The King of Completion Rates

Sadness-driven videos don't get instant likes, but they keep viewers watching until the very end. Silent Diary is the perfect example of this.

**The formula:** Build empathy → Accumulate emotion → Trigger with one small detail

The key to sadness isn't "making people cry" — it's "making people remember themselves." I broke down an 8-million-view Silent Diary video with a simple structure: someone can't sleep at night → scrolls through phone photos → stops on an old picture → close-up of a finger hovering over the screen. Four steps. No dialogue. No melodramatic narration.

**Music:** Cello or slow piano, 60-70 bpm
**Visuals:** Cool tones, slow camera movement, plenty of empty space

## Anger — The Engagement Magnifier

What does the comments section of an anger-driven video look like? People are either angry at the person in the video or angry at someone in the comments. Anger drives the highest interaction rate of all six emotions — but it needs to be used carefully.

**Formula:** Show an injustice → Point out the problem → Take a clear stance

I made a video exposing "fake bargaining" in livestreams. It opened with: "Have you ever seen an anchor do this?" Then I recreated how sellers stage the whole performance — price tag 1999, cut to 199, "we're LOSING money today, guys." That video got more comments in 24 hours than everything I'd posted in the previous month combined.

**Music:** Heavy drums or alert sounds, strong rhythm
**Visuals:** Quick cuts, contrasting shots, red/black color palette

## Fear — The Completion Rate Booster

Fear doesn't mean "scary." It means creating an information gap — viewers don't know what's coming next, and that uncertainty spikes completion rates.

**The formula:** Present a threat → Build suspense → Offer a solution

"Your bank account is losing 3 yuan every single day. Did you even notice?" I tested this opener and got a 5-second retention rate above 85%. The secret was layering information progressively and always ending with a fix — otherwise viewers will roast you.

**Music:** Suspenseful sound effects, escalating rhythm
**Visuals:** Dark tones, key information highlighted with text overlays

## Disgust — The Most Controversial Trigger

Disgust-driven videos often feature images or facts people find uncomfortable — and precisely because of that discomfort, viewers feel compelled to share it so "others can see too."

**The formula:** Show something abnormal → Explain why → Give practical advice

A video I made exposing "cheap suit fabric quality" compared two cross-sections of fabric — one with neat stitching, one covered in loose threads and glue. It ended with three simple ways to spot quality fabric. The video blew up for no obvious reason at first, but looking back, "disgust + practical value" created a perfect emotional-and-utility combination.

**Music:** Neutral or theatrical effects
**Visuals:** Comparative composition, macro close-ups

## Surprise — The Fastest Emotional Spike

Surprise is the quickest emotion to hit and the fastest to fade. It works best as a "hook" rather than the main emotional thread.

**The formula:** Break expectations → Reveal the truth → End briefly

"I spent 2000 yuan on a custom suit, and then the tailor measured me wrong..." The first three seconds shattered the viewer's expectations. The key was delivering value after the surprise, or viewers feel clickbaited.

**Music:** Sudden audio change, "WOW"-type effects
**Visuals:** Fast transitions, high contrast

## The Emotion Combination Strategy

The real masters don't rely on just one emotion. Here's the combination model I've refined:

| Video Type | First Emotion | Second Emotion | Best Use Case |
|-----------|-------------|--------------|-------------|
| Reversal | Surprise | Joy | Everyday surprises |
| Empathy | Sadness | Gratitude | Silent Diary-style |
| Exposé | Anger | Surprise | Industry secrets |
| Growth | Fear | Joy | Knowledge sharing |
| Healing | Sadness | Calm | Late-night alone content |

After all this time making emotional short videos, my biggest realization is this: people don't scroll through videos to "learn something." They scroll to *feel* something. If your video makes them feel — even if that feeling is anger or disgust — that's infinitely more powerful than leaving them with no feeling at all.

These six emotions are like paint on a palette. You don't need to use every color every time. But knowing what each one does is what separates the artists from the amateurs.
