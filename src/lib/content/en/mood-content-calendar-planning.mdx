# Full-Year Emotional Content Calendar: Spring, Summer, Fall, Winter — Just Follow Along

If you've made emotional short videos for more than a week, you know the feeling: the first two days you're on fire, cranking out three videos in a row. Day four hits and you're stuck. "What do I even film today?" You spend half an hour scrolling for ideas, settle on something random, hit publish, and the data is a disaster.

This isn't your fault. It means you don't have a system.

My approach to content planning is simple: map out the emotional rhythms of the year, pre-slot your topics, and then just execute whatever's on the calendar. No thinking required.

This is a continuation of the same practical methodology from my earlier articles. No fluff — just the 52-week topic template you can use tomorrow.

## Emotions Follow Seasonal Patterns

There's nothing mystical about it. Humans experience predictable emotional cycles throughout the year. It's psychology, plain and simple.

| Season | Core Emotion | Hot Topics | Best Style |
|:-------|:------------|:-----------|:-----------|
| Spring (Jan–Mar) | New year anxiety / Goal-setting / Fresh start | Procrastination, plans falling apart, the feeling of returning home | Healing, warmth |
| Summer (Apr–Jun) | Graduation / Parting / Career anxiety | Interview nerves, breakup season, "I need to get my life together" | Nostalgia, struggle |
| Fall (Jul–Sep) | Job pressure / Self-doubt / Loneliness | Hiring season, renting, long-distance relationships, roommate drama | Resonance, monologue |
| Winter (Oct–Dec) | Year-end review / Family / Holiday anxiety | Marriage pressure, annual recap, new year resolutions | Reflection, family warmth |

See the pattern? You're not "creating" emotional topics. Your audience is already feeling these emotions at these specific times. All you're doing is saying the words they're already thinking.

I posted a video about "going home for Chinese New Year and getting pressured about marriage" in late February. It did three times better than a similar video I posted in August. Not because August doesn't have marriage pressure — but because in February everyone was living it. The resonance was at its peak.

## Month-by-Month Emotional Topic Planner

Here's the monthly planner I use. Four fixed directions per month. Follow this and you'll never run out of ideas.

**January: New Year + Downward Spiral**

Everyone makes resolutions on January 1st. But what actually resonates emotionally is "last year's resolutions that failed." Two angles: hopeful new year content ("this year, I'll learn to love myself") and new year anxiety content ("I'm 30 and still alone"). Post the former around New Year's Day. Post the latter mid-January, when the resolutions start crumbling and the anxiety is at its peak.

**February: Homecoming + Family**

Spring Festival travel season is the emotional peak for family content. The anxiety of buying tickets, the anticipation of going home, the awkwardness of not knowing how to talk to your parents. The hottest emotional topic this month: "coming home after being away for so long, everything feels unfamiliar." Year-end reunions with old classmates are also a goldmine — comparison anxiety, unexpected encounters.

**March: Back to Work + City Life**

The workforce returns to the city and the grind resumes. Traffic jams, overtime, apartment hunting — all high-resonance material. March also carries an emotional undercurrent: spring is here, but for many people winter hasn't left their hearts. "Everyone else is getting better, but I'm still stuck" — this theme absolutely crushes in March.

**April: Pre-Graduation + Nostalgia**

Two months until graduation, but the emotions have already started. Especially for juniors and seniors — anxiety and nostalgia tangled together. "I never realized how much my student ID was worth" / "the last class of university" — these themes perform ridiculously well in April. The Qingming Festival (Tomb-Sweeping Day) nostalgia angle works too — not just for the departed, but for the past in general.

**May: Love Season + Breakup Season**

May is the most concentrated month for relationship emotions. The joy of confessing love on one side, the peak of breakups on the other. The week around May 20th (520, Chinese Valentine's Day) is competitive, but if your content is good, it doesn't matter. One of my May videos — "the stupidest thing you ever did for love" — ran up 600,000 plays. Timing was everything.

**June: Graduation + Goodbye**

The true explosion of graduation emotions. Prom pictures, farewell dinners, sending friends off at the train station. Emotional content in June has a built-in traffic multiplier — millions of university students are at peak emotional sensitivity. "From now on, hometown only has summer and winter, never spring or fall again" — this line goes viral every single June.

**July: New Grads + Apartment Hunting**

Graduates flood into the city. Rent struggles, job hunt struggles, the first night alone in a new apartment. "My first time renting by myself, I realized I didn't even know how to change a water jug" — these topics absolutely work in July because they're the real, lived experience of the audience.

**August: Summer + Loneliness**

Summer carries a unique kind of loneliness. Everyone else is traveling, posting on social media, living their best life. You're on your phone in a stuffy rented room. "Summer is almost over and I've done nothing" — this hits hardest in late August.

**September: Back to School + Career "Fresh Start"**

September isn't just back-to-school season for students. It's also a reset moment for a lot of working people. The golden hiring season (Golden September, Silver October) kicks off. People switching jobs, switching cities, starting new relationships. "It's autumn. Time to start over in a new city" — this topic lands perfectly in September.

**October: National Holiday + Relationship Tension**

The week-long National Day holiday amplifies every relationship dynamic. Some couples grow closer through travel. Others break up during it. "We came back from the trip and broke up" — this story template sees a mini traffic spike every single October. Also, the "post-holiday blues" angle is a strong filler topic.

**November: Year-End Anxiety + Double 11**

Double 11 isn't just a shopping festival anymore. It's an emotional amplifier. "The things I can afford are getting fewer and fewer" / "cart is full but my heart is still empty" — the gap between spending power and desire is the emotional center this month. On top of that, with two months left in the year, many people start feeling the anxiety: "another year gone."

**December: Year-End + Countdown to Home**

Annual reviews, new year plans, countdown to going home. December's emotional atmosphere is a warm anticipation mixed with a thread of anxiety. "I didn't do enough this year" / "next year has to be better" — this contradictory emotional state peaks in late December.

## Tracking Social Sentiment Events

Beyond the fixed seasonal topics, there's another major source: social sentiment events.

What counts as a social sentiment event? Something the entire internet is discussing, with emotion running high. A graduation speech that goes viral. A celebrity breakup. A social news controversy. These events carry their own traffic and their emotional density is off the charts.

How do you find them? My daily routine takes exactly 10 minutes: scan Weibo trending topics, Douyin hot list, and WeChat Search suggestions. Each platform's trending list has a different flavor — Weibo is social news, Douyin is pop culture, WeChat Search is deep interest topics. All three together give you complete coverage of any emotion-potential topic.

When I spot a potential topic, I ask three questions:

1. Is this "I've felt this too" or "what does this have to do with me"? The former is worth following, the latter isn't.
2. Is this emotion on the rise or already past peak? If it's rising, jump in. If it's topped out, skip it.
3. How strong is the resonance with my specific audience? If only a subset resonates, judge whether that subset is big enough.

Real example: last September, a news story about a 23-year-old woman who died from overwork sparked nationwide debate about overtime culture. I didn't directly cover the news (too heavy, attracts backlash). Instead, I made an emotional video about "not wanting to reply to messages after work." Never mentioned the news. But the feeling of "being held hostage by work" matched the sentiment in the air. That video performed well too.

## Building Your Own Content Calendar

Finally, here's how to actually set this up in Feishu or Excel. Don't over-engineer it. A simple table is all you need.

Here's the template I use:

| Month | Week | Emotional Theme | Keywords | Format | Publish Date |
|:------|:-----|:---------------|:---------|:-------|:------------|
| May | Week 3 | Crush | too scared to confess, stolen glances, coincidences | Silent Diary style | May 17 |
| May | Week 4 | Graduation memories | dorm room, last meal together | Narration + visuals | May 24 |
| June | Week 1 | Goodbye | seeing off friends, going separate ways | First-person monologue | June 1 |

Four steps, that's it:

**Step 1:** List the seasonal emotions for all 12 months (use my monthly table above).

**Step 2:** For each month, break down 4-5 specific topic keywords based on that month's emotional theme.

**Step 3:** Assign each topic to a specific week and a specific format.

**Step 4:** One week ahead, write the script and prep the assets. When the date comes, just post.

My habit: on the 1st of each month, I schedule 4-5 topics for the month. Then I spend two hours each week batch-producing two videos — one for Sunday, one for Wednesday. Two videos a week is enough to stay active without burning out.

A lot of people who make emotional content eventually hit a wall with consistent output. It's not a skill problem. It's a system problem. Turn your topics into a calendar, and you stop waiting for inspiration. You just do what's on the sheet.

I stuck with this for three months, and the biggest change I noticed was this: content creation became an assembly line. The anxiety dropped. The output stabilized. That feeling alone made the whole thing worth it.
